Looking at katydids

I know that two-species comparisons are bad (Garland and Adolph 1994) and even if they work the species should be pretty closely related1 Cox and Logan (2021), but I do think there are two species that exemplify two possible ends of the Allee signaling hypothesis.2 Common species use more short-range signals, because they have more potential receivers within range and are at more risk from eavesdroppers.

Beauteous!

The species are Ischnomela pulchripennis3 from Central America and Schedocentrus differens, both of subfamily Pseudophyllinae. Ischnomela pulchripennis is rare (at least in Panama; it seemed more common in Costa Rica at La Selva station) and, as reported by Belwood (1988), produces unusally long, broadband airborne calls and few short range calls, switching to tremulation mostly only when disturbed. In contrast, Schedocentrus differens4 from Ecuador, is known to only produce tremulations and drums, apparently does not have a calling song, and is very common, as reported by Morris et al. (1994). These patterns, though pretty much anecdotal, fit with what we would expect for species that are able to “get away with” using (or “need” to use) longer range or more conspicuous signals because of their rarity.
